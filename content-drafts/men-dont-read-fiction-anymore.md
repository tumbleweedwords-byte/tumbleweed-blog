# I Am the Only Man I Know Who Still Reads Novels

Last Tuesday I gave a copy of Denis Johnson's *Train Dreams* to a friend in a pub in Leith.

He is in his forties. Works in construction. Reads voraciously. History, mostly. A bit of true crime. The odd biography. His bookshelf at home has about three hundred books on it. Not one of them is a novel.

I handed him the Johnson and said, "You'll love this. It's short. It's the best thing I've read this year."

He looked at the cover. Turned it over. Read the back. Then he said something I have been turning over in my head for the last week.

"Is it made up?"

## The last real men's novel was published in 2012

I told him yes, it was made up. Then I watched his face change. The interest went out of it. He put the book down on the table and ordered another pint and changed the subject.

This is the pattern I keep running into. Men I know, men I like, men whose opinions I trust on everything from architecture to football, do not read novels. Not because they cannot. Not because they hate reading. My friend reads more pages in a month than most people do in a year. They just do not read fiction.

The data backs this up. Women make up roughly eighty per cent of literary fiction readers in the UK and US, depending on which survey you trust. Literary festivals run at about four women to every man in the audience. The average fiction buyer is a woman in her fifties. The figures have not always looked like this. In the 1980s and 90s the split was much closer, around sixty-forty. By 2010 it was seventy-thirty. By 2020 it was eighty-twenty. The gap is still widening.

The easy version of this conversation goes one of two ways. The reactionary version says men are being erased from literature, that publishers have turned against them, that the novels written for men are no longer being published. The dismissive version says good, men should read more, it is their own fault, let them read history books and stew in it.

I am not interested in either of those takes. They both miss what is actually happening.

## What happened was boring

What happened was boring. It was not a conspiracy. It was not a plot. It was a slow cultural drift that nobody pushed and nobody stopped.

The last generation of novelists who commanded a wide male readership were the ones men read in the 80s and 90s. Updike. Roth. Bellow. Richard Ford. Cormac McCarthy. Martin Amis. Ian McEwan in his early career. Don DeLillo. Jonathan Franzen just at the edge of it. Men read these writers because their fathers read them, because they were in the newspapers their fathers bought, because the books were in the shops they walked past on the way to work.

When those writers retired or died or just stopped producing the kind of book their readers wanted, nobody replaced them in the same cultural position. Not because the new writers were worse. Some of the new writers are astonishing. The problem was that the infrastructure that used to put those books in front of men quietly disappeared at the same time.

The broadsheet newspapers that ran three thousand word book reviews on a Saturday morning have cut their books coverage by about seventy per cent since 2005. The bookshops in small towns closed. The literary programmes on Radio 4 got shorter and rarer. The lad mags that occasionally ran a piece on a new novel are all gone. The Sunday supplement your dad used to read over coffee still exists but the book section is one page and half of it is about celebrity memoirs.

Men in the 80s did not seek out literary fiction. Literary fiction came looking for them. When it stopped coming looking, most men stopped reading it, not because they decided to but because they had no idea what to read.

## What men actually read now

They read a lot. Just not novels.

I have done the informal survey over the last year. I have asked every man I know what he has read recently. The answers come back in clusters.

History. Tom Holland, Mary Beard, Dan Jones, Peter Frankopan. Big narrative history with strong male narrators.

Biography, especially military or political. The new Churchill, the new Napoleon, the new Thatcher.

True crime and unsolved mysteries. David Grann is the crossover king here. *Killers of the Flower Moon*, *The Wager*. Men will read literary prose if it is attached to a real story.

Stoicism and self-improvement. Ryan Holiday, Jordan Peterson, Mark Manson. I am not going to pretend this is a surprise.

Thrillers and spy novels, occasionally. Le Carre holdouts. The new Mick Herron. Robert Harris.

Sports memoir, finance, business, popular science. Adam Kay's medical books. Bill Bryson still sells to men.

What is missing from all of those is the thing that literary fiction alone does. Literary fiction is the only form that spends time inside another human being's head and shows you what it is like in there. History tells you what happened. Biography tells you what someone did. Stoicism tells you what to think. Literary fiction is the only form that says, here is what it feels like to be her, here is what it feels like to be him, here is what it feels like to be someone you would never become.

## What is lost

This is the part I keep coming back to. Not as a sermon. As a fact.

Reading fiction is the only widely available practice that trains a reader in sustained empathy for a person they have never met. It is the place where you spend hours in the head of a woman, a Black American, a Korean grandmother, a Glasgow pensioner, a depressed teenage girl, a dying man. Other art forms do this too, but none of them do it at the scale or depth of the novel. Film is shorter. Television is external. Poetry is compressed. Only the novel gives you three hundred pages inside another consciousness.

When men stop reading novels, they lose that practice. Not immediately. Not catastrophically. But over a generation the practice drains out of a culture, and what remains is a population that knows what other people do but not what other people feel. That is a rougher and less patient culture than the one before it.

This is not a "men should read more" piece. I do not think scolding works. I think the real question is softer and harder than that. It is whether the infrastructure that used to put novels in front of men can be rebuilt, and if so, what that would look like. I do not have a clean answer. I have only what I can do myself.

## What I actually do about it

I keep giving men books. I do not push. I hand over a short novel, say "this is a good one," and walk away. About one in four of them reads it. About one in eight finishes it and then wants to talk about it. The ratio is not great but it is not zero.

The book I give men more than any other is *Train Dreams* by Denis Johnson. One hundred and sixteen pages. A man's life in the American West. Wolves and trains and a wife and a loss. No sentimentality. Every line earned. It is the book I give because it is short enough to finish and honest enough to land.

I gave it to my friend in the Leith pub last Tuesday. He put it on the table and ordered another pint and we talked about the football.

On Saturday morning he sent me a text. It said: "Read it. Twice. Fuck. Send me another."

That is the only review I have ever needed.
